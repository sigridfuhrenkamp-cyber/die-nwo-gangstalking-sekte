# The sound of disinformation: TikTok, computational propaganda, and the invasion of Ukraine
**Source:** New Media & Society (Sage), 2024  
**URL:** https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/14614448241251804  
**DOI:** 10.1177/14614448241251804  
**Authors:** Marcus Bösch, Tom Divon

## Abstract
TikTok has emerged as a powerful platform for the dissemination of mis- and disinformation about the war in Ukraine. In the first three months after the Russian invasion (Feb 2022), videos under #Ukraine garnered **36.9 billion views**, with individual videos up to 88 million views. Beyond images and text, **sound** has emerged as a novel, platform-specific technique. The analysis distinguishes war-related sounds used by Ukraine and Russia and classifies them into a mis- and disinformation typology. Using **computational propaganda** features—automation, scalability, anonymity—the article explores how TikTok’s auditory practices exacerbate information disorders: reusing sounds for coordinated campaigns, creating audio meme templates for rapid amplification, and deleting original sounds to conceal orchestrators’ identities. TikTok’s recommendation system ("for you" page) acts as a **sound space** where semi-automated "soft" propaganda thrives via audio features.

## Key concepts
- **Computational propaganda:** "use of algorithms, automation, and human curation to purposefully manage and distribute misleading information" (Woolley & Howard 2019)
- **Sound spaces:** automation-based environments where persuasion and influence thrive through auditory practices
- **Audio memes:** short sound clips (songs, movies) reused and varied; "use this sound" links to sound pages aggregating all videos with that audio
- **Six types** of audio-based mis/disinformation (Wardle): from low (satire, false connection) to high (manipulated, fabricated content)

## Examples in paper
- Alex Hook (Ukrainian soldier) dancing to "Smooth Criminal" etc.; 88M views in 24h; #AlexHook challenge 476M views; pro-Ukrainian narrative
- Bayraktar song (Ukrainian propaganda); remixes across genres; demonisation of enemy; scalability via "reuse sound"
